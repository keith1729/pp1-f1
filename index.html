Inside the world of F1

Marvels of Engineering

Aerodynamics: F1 cars feature intricate aerodynamic designs to maximize downforce and minimize drag. The front and rear wings, bargeboards, and diffusers all play crucial roles in shaping airflow around the car.

Chassis: The monocoque chassis is made of lightweight carbon fiber and houses the driver. It provides structural integrity and safety. The suspension system, including push-rod or pull-rod setups, ensures optimal handling.

Power Unit (PU): Internal Combustion Engine (ICE): A turbocharged V6 engine with hybrid technology. It produces around 800 horsepower. Energy Recovery Systems (ERS): MGU-K (Motor Generator Unit - Kinetic): Converts kinetic energy during braking into electrical energy stored in the battery. MGU-H (Motor Generator Unit - Heat): Recovers energy from exhaust gases. Energy Store (ES): The battery that stores electrical energy. Control Electronics (CE): Manages energy flow. The PU delivers a total power output of approximately 1000 horsepower.

Transmission: F1 cars use seamless-shift gearboxes with 8 forward gears and 1 reverse. Gear changes happen in milliseconds.

Tires: Pirelli provides specialized tires for F1. They have different compounds (soft, medium, hard) for varying track conditions.

Brakes: Carbon-ceramic brakes withstand extreme temperatures. Brake-by-wire systems allow precise control.

Fuel: F1 cars use high-octane racing fuel. Fuel efficiency is critical due to race regulations.
